Once graduated, I assigned for a Physics Faculty of a KNU and for some time I was absolutely thrilled by a magic of physics and beautiful mathmatics behind it. Both of these are as spellbinding, as nature itself, if you know where to search.

Games

But it was all far from serious, until I discovered Minecraft. Provided I loved to play games in first place, a sandbox game with infinite world, immesurable building capabilities and adaptive gameplay just blew my mind, and I was in constant search for even a slightest moment to enjoy it.

Code

Logicaly, provided my STEM-oriented mind, I was introduced to coding back in school. For a long time I had a purely math-related interest in it, so once accomodated with a basic PASCAL syntax, I was set.

But at some point, I discovered mods for Minecraft. It tool some time to understand that, but apparently even the most advanced and breathtaking of them were usually written by a single person. At that point, I realised: If someone can do it, then I can probably too? After investigation, I found that mods and Minecraft itself are written in Java, and I just started to learn it, bit-by-bit. Yes, Jav was my first Object-Oriented Language, and I absolutely don't regret that - for me Java prooved to be more intuitive, but for you of course it may not. The concepts of OOP suppeenly clicked switch in my head: previously Minecraft seemed to me like some sort of magic that noone but chosen can master. After that, it all become trivial to me: you break the program into small pieces (methods), group them (objects) and use them to your liking. These ideas might be trivial, but the behaviour a code may have in case you use them is infinitely complex. But in essence, this complex structure is simple bricks, arranged in simple patterns, creating in simple shapes.
